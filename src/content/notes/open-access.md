I have long been an advocate for open access to research. It was only during my PhD that I realised that the idea that science benefitted society was not quite true. Regularly we get the media explaining to us new advances and reports on issues and risks. We assume that a) they are trained in that specific discipline, at least enough to be able to understand the research and b) they actually have read the original research (rather than just a press release).

However, if you wanted to dig into the truths of a piece of research, say as an educator who wants to bring cutting-edge science to their class, you cannot do so without paying. A lot. And we have already paid for it through our taxes!

Further, when in research I knew that if you contacted a researcher directly, they would give you a paper. It's not like a band or an author, the creator of the work does not get any money from it, no royalties. In fact, they would want their work to *have* the biggest audience!

Back in 2001 I proposed this for a mooted Essex Education Forum (on the day of 9/11!). Later, I was invited to a Royal Society Symposium representing the secondary science teaching profession to argue that schools *need* to have a way of accessing peer reviewed work freely.

Since the early 2000s I am pleased that we are moving towards open access publications. But there is still too much that is blocked, meaning that we *aren't* able to use research in the way that it was already intended.

Of course, there are always other ways you can access research that should in reality be free to the public…
