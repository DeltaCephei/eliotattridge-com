A moth lands on the porch light. I take a photo, mostly because the patterning is unfamiliar. I upload it to iNaturalist. The computer-vision suggestion offers four candidates with confidences. I pick the one that matches the forewing, and within a day a curator I've never met confirms it. It is now a verified record. It will sit in a database. It might, in some small way, contribute to a range map.

The chain from "thing on porch" to "verified record" is short, mostly invisible, and quietly remarkable. I've been thinking about why it feels so satisfying.

## What's actually happening

Three things are stacking on top of each other, and each one used to be its own profession.

1. **Identification.** Once needed a key, a hand lens, and someone older than you who knew the local fauna. Now needs a phone camera and a model trained on millions of labelled images.
2. **Documentation.** Once meant a notebook, a date, a location, ideally a sketch. Now happens automatically as image metadata.
3. **Verification.** Once meant sending a specimen to an expert and waiting weeks. Now means a curator clicking "agree" within hours.

Each step is now cheap. The cost was the bottleneck that kept amateur natural history mostly amateur. The cost being low means the labour is now mostly attention. Whether you noticed the moth at all.

## What I keep noticing

The thing I keep noticing is that the technology hasn't replaced the noticing. It's amplified it. The moth at the light is the same moth it would have been in 1985. What's changed is that paying attention now produces a permanent, shareable, legible record almost for free.

This is the same shape as the AI tutoring story, actually. The bottleneck wasn't capability. The bottleneck was cost. Once the cost drops, the constraint moves somewhere else. In the moth case, it moves to attention. In the tutoring case, it moves to curriculum design and human relationship.

Worth pulling on this thread.

## Recent records

A small list of moths I've identified at the porch light this autumn. I'll keep adding to it.

- **Plusia oxygramma**, the porina moth. Common, but the wing pattern is gorgeous up close.
- An unfamiliar geometrid I'm still arguing about with iNaturalist's model.
- *Wiseana cervinata*, larger than I expected and apparently pestilential to pasture grasses.
