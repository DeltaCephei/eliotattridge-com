Benjamin Bloom's 1984 claim was that students who got one-to-one mastery tutoring outperformed conventionally taught students by two standard deviations. Two sigma. The average tutored student ended up around the 98th percentile of the class average<Ref n={1} />. He called it a problem because the result was real but not scalable: you couldn't put a personal tutor next to every learner.

The claim has been the gravitational centre of personalised-learning research for forty years.

It's also been quietly falling apart.

## The replication trouble

The two-sigma figure rests on a small number of small studies, mostly in primary mathematics, with mastery learning baked in alongside the tutoring. Recent meta-analyses put the pure tutoring effect closer to 0.3 to 0.7 sigma depending on what you're counting<Ref n={2} />. Still substantial. Not two.

Bloom himself was clearer about this than most of the people who quote him. He framed it as a target, not an established quantity. The "problem" was always: *if* one-to-one is this powerful, how do we get something close to it at scale.

## What AI tutoring changes

The thing AI tutoring quietly changes is not the answer to the two-sigma question. It's the question itself.

For forty years the question was: how do we make personalised tutoring cheap enough to scale? The 2023 onwards RCTs on AI tutors are small but interesting<Ref n={3} />, and the early results land in roughly the same 0.3 to 0.7 sigma range as well-designed human tutoring. Which means the cost-of-scale problem is now answered, and the question becomes a different one:

> Now that personalised feedback is essentially free, what is school *for*?

That's a much less comfortable question. It's also a much more interesting one. The two-sigma framing made personalised learning a logistical problem. The new framing makes it a curriculum and purpose problem.

## What I'd want to read next

- A clean RCT comparing AI tutoring to a wait-list control in a senior secondary science context, ideally NCEA-aligned. The existing studies skew younger and more US-centric.
- Anything that takes seriously the difference between feedback that closes a gap and feedback that builds metacognition. AI is good at the first. The second is still mostly human.
