The argument goes like this. If you write down a small idea today, and you write down another small idea next week, and a third the week after, and you let them sit somewhere they can find each other, then in a year you don't have 52 ideas. You have 52 ideas plus whatever happened when they touched.

It's a quietly radical claim. The labour is linear. The output isn't.

The honest version of the claim is more careful. Most notes never touch anything. Most ideas die in the same week they're written. The compounding is real but it's heavy-tailed and you only see it in retrospect, when an old half-finished thought suddenly explains a brand-new observation.

That's the thing this site is for. Building the surface area for the unlikely connection.

## What I notice in my own notes

When I look back at Tana from a year ago, the notes that ended up mattering are not the ones I thought would matter at the time. The ones I worked hardest on are usually fine but inert. The ones that turn out to compound are the ones that were small, slightly weird, and tagged with something I couldn't quite name.

I think the lesson is: don't optimise for the *quality* of any individual note. Optimise for the *number of small, weird, slightly-misshapen* notes you make. Then make it easy for them to find each other.

That second part is the bit that's actually hard.
