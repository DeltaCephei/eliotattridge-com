I went back into the confidence-weighted multiple choice (CWMCT) literature this week because I've been quietly wondering if a low-stakes version would help my Year 12 biology class think more honestly about what they actually know.

The short version: the technique has been around since the 1930s. Researchers have been trying to make multiple choice tests measure something deeper than recognition for nearly a century, and CWMCT is one of the longer-running attempts. It asks students to mark not just an answer but how confident they are in it, then scores them in a way that rewards calibration as well as correctness.

The long version is messier than I expected.

## What it actually measures

Done well, CWMCT measures three things at once:

- The student's knowledge.
- Their metacognition, that is, how well they know what they know.
- Their willingness to commit. This is the part nobody talks about, and it's a confound.

The third one matters because the students who tick "very confident" most often are not always the most knowledgeable. They are sometimes the most confident *as a personality trait*. There are gender effects here that are well documented and worth being careful about. Pretending the technique is neutral is a mistake.

## Why I'm interested anyway

Because the alternative, in the classroom, is what we have now. Standard MCQs reward guessing, give no signal about metacognition, and let a student walk away with "I got 14 out of 20" without ever knowing whether they knew it or guessed it. CWMCT at least surfaces that distinction.

For revision, not for high-stakes assessment, it might be exactly the right shape. The student finds out which questions they were confidently wrong about. Those are the ones to study.

## Open questions for the trial

1. What scoring rule? The classic ones (Brier, log) are mathematically nice but not student-friendly. A simpler 1-2-3 confidence scale with bonus and penalty is more legible but loses the calibration story.
2. Do I tell students the scoring rule in advance? Yes, obviously. But how much explanation lands without losing them?
3. Does this work across topics or only on factual recall? My instinct is the latter. Worth testing.
4. Is the metacognitive benefit *durable* or only present during the test itself?

I'll write this up properly after the trial.
